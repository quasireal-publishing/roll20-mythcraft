The world of adventure is rife with far more dangers than direct person-to-person battle. You might face an engine of war, such as a trebuchet. Terrible blows might break bone or spirit, leaving you to contend with a lengthy recovery. Poisons and diseases lurk in moldering dungeons, cursed crypts, and even stinking alleys. Devious villains might even implement such maladies as weapons in their arsenal.


This chapter offers a few examples of siege equipment, lingering injuries, poisons and diseases, and insanity. You can use these as a frame of reference for creating your own.

Structure Health

The durability of structures, such as bridges, houses, and castles, may come into play at some point on an epic quest, especially in a siege scenario. When determining the HP, armor rating (AR), and the damage threshold (DT) of a structure, use the following table as a guideline. Take into account the specific circumstances of your region. If a castle wall has eroded under high winds or heavy downpours for years already, then consider lowering any of its stats to account for this. Conversely, if a bridge is magically reinforced, then it might have a higher Resist against physical damage or might be Immune to nonmagical damage.


Structural integrity should typically be tracked in 10 ft by 10 ft panels. As the MC, it is your job to rule how the destruction of a portion of a structure impacts the rest of the structure. Knocking a 10 ft square hole in the side of a stone wall might cause the wall above it to crumble, or the wall portion above the hole might remain in place, held firm by the intact walls on either side.


Note that any damage reduction (DR) is applied after determining DT. All inanimate materials are Immune to psychic damage.

Damage Reduction (DR)

If a creature or structure has Damage Reduction (DR) X, they subtract their DR number from all damage they take, unless they are Vulnerable to the type of damage that they are taking. If damage specifies that it ignores Resist, it also ignores DR. The DR number may be followed by a weapon tag, magic source, or damage type. This means that creature takes full damage from a weapon with that tag or damage type.


For example, a creature with DR 5/Fire would take full damage from fire, and subtract 5 from all other sources of damage. A creature with DR 15/Silvered would take full damage from silvered weapons, and subtract 15 from all other damage.

Damage Threshold (DT)

Damage Threshold (DT) is a very rare defense that is typically seen on structures and vehicles. DT determines how much damage a creature must deal at once to deal any damage at all.


For example, if a stone wall has DT 15, and a player deals 14 damage to it, it instead takes 0. If a player deals 16 damage, conversely, then the stone wall takes all 16 damage because its DT 15 was overcome.


For the purposes of overcoming DT, “damage dealt at once” is determined by a single expenditure of AP. Each expenditure of AP for an attack is a new roll against DT; you cannot stack attacks to overcome DT.. SIEGE EQUIPMENT

Castles and fortified towns are usually well-defended, and arrows might not suffice to penetrate thick stone walls. Whether your heroes purchase and use siege equipment or must fight against it, these machines can significantly increase the tension in a combat scene.


Siege equipment is a little like the weapons and armor that an adventurer might use, but has more intricacies.

RANGED

A typical siege weapon will have the Ranged property, which is functionally the same as any weapon’s Ranged property.

AMMUNITION

All nonmagical siege weapons will have the Ammunition tag. Unlike normal weapons, a number of AP will be listed immediately after the ammunition type. It takes that many AP to load a siege weapon. A siege weapon cannot make a ranged attack if it is not loaded.


A siege weapon lists the type of ammunition it uses: a ball or a bolt. A standard ball or bolt will deal the damage listed by the weapon, but specialty ammunition will have other effects.

RELOAD

Many siege weapons have a Reload feature. After it is fired, a creature must spend the appropriate number of AP to set the weapon for usage again, whereupon ammunition can be applied.

AREA OF EFFECT

Ammunition fired from a siege weapon generally causes a shockwave of blunt force, a hail of sharp blades, or an explosion of fire or other elemental energy. The AOE column specifies a radius (from the point of contact) or a line or cone (drawn from the siege weapon) to a target point within range. The attack is made against every creature in that area.

HEFT AND SPEED

Siege equipment is large and cumbersome. A Heft score provides an abstract way of measuring the amount of force it takes to move that equipment.


When a number of creatures collectively pull or push the equipment, they must spend 3 of their AP on a turn to move it. A creature must count as Medium or larger for the purposes of lifting, dragging, and carrying to contribute. When a creature contributes to moving a siege engine, add its STR to a collective pool. At the start of its next turn, the creature removes its STR from the pool unless it uses its turn to move the engine again.


If a humanoid counts as Large or larger for the purposes of lifting, dragging, and carrying, they can add double their STR.


When the collective pool of STR meets or exceeds ½ of a siege engine’s Heft score, the creatures that contributed may move the equipment up to ½ its speed; if they achieve a total of equal to or greater than the equipment’s Heft, then it moves its total speed. Any creature that contributed to moving the equipment immediately moves with it when this occurs.

HEALTH AND ARMOR

Siege equipment has HP, AR, REF, and FORT just like any creature. Most siege equipment also has DT or other forms of resistance to damage. It does not have ANT, LOG, or WILL, however, and cannot be affected by any ability or attack that would target those defenses.

ATTACK BONUS

When making an attack with a siege weapon, choose AWR or INT. For every 3 points you have in that attribute, add +1 to your attack roll. Additionally, for every 4 points you have in the Vehicles* [land] skill, add +1 to your attack roll.


This will generally mean that attacks with a siege weapon have much lower modifiers than attacks with a normal weapon. This is intentional; siege weapons are devastating when used on easy-to-hit targets such as buildings, but are not as effective against individual targets. Furthermore, many siege weapons still deal half damage even if they miss.

CRIT FAILS

Siege engines are very particular mechanisms and many things can go wrong with them. They crit fail on a roll of 1 or 2. When they crit fail, they no longer function until they are repaired by a craftsperson with the relevant skill set.

TRAPS

When implemented judiciously, traps can be an excellent way to keep adventurers on their toes. Traps come in many forms, and not all traps will deal damage. Some might ensnare the heroes or alert the denizens of a dungeon to the heroes’ presence, for example.


Traps that do deal damage should typically deal about as much damage as a hero might suffer in a combat encounter. This is not a hard and fast rule, however; some very well hidden traps might be very difficult to spot, and their damage might be lethal.

The Overcoming DC does not necessarily represent you disarming the trap. It might represent a hero jumping over a gap, discovering a secret door, finding the correct lever or keyhole, or figuring out the correct password to bypass the danger.


Traps might be triggered in many ways if the Perceiving DC is not met. Displacing a door or object might set off a trap mechanism. A pressure-sensitive floor plate might have light objects resting on it, giving the illusion of safety, but when an average adult humanoid steps on it, their weight triggers the trap. Hidden trapdoors and tripwires are common traps that are low-effort but highly effective. A false keyhole or misguiding lever might look like it will help you progress through the dungeon only to spring a trap on you instead. Your imagination is the only limit for designing how traps are concealed and how they might be overcome.


In addition to traditional traps that drop heroes into pits or hit them with blades or darts of some kind, consider more unique traps. Poisonous gas, explosions, a magical rune that teleports a hero into some kind of prison, falling rocks, and a magical monster spawning portal are all unique ideas that can make for memorable traps.

LINGERING INJURIES

Sometimes, a hero will sustain a significant injury. A club from a trollkin berzerker might crack a rib, or an unfortunate encounter with a swarm of bendelexes might leave an adventurer with severe internal bleeding.


Giving a hero a lingering injury can be a meaningful way to remind the heroes that they are not immortal, and that sometimes they are not up to the task of defeating every monster they encounter. Note, however, that lingering injuries can seriously inhibit a hero’s ability to function and should only be used sparingly. As a frame of reference, you might considering lingering injuries if:


  • A major villain or boss monster crits
  • A hero takes damage equal to or greater than ½ their maximum HP
  • The heroes confront a monster that they have been told is far too powerful for them to face

POISON

Poisons come in many forms, and might be applied to a weapon, ingested with a meal, or breathed in while exploring a deadly dungeon.


Poisons are categorized with three types of tags:


  • Rarity denotes how common a poison is. Poison will be Common, Uncommon, Rare, Very Rare, or Mythical.
  • Lethality denotes how dangerous a poison is. Lower-lethality poisons are typically much more common.
  • Transmissibility denotes how likely the effects of the poison are to spread to another target. High transmissibility is very uncommon for poisons.

Several abilities might allow you to ignore or treat poisons of a certain level of rarity. Lethality and transmissibility are not referenced in the Core Rulebook, but will come into play in later releases as the peoples of Ancerra become more medically advanced.


Poison stat blocks have three parts: Application, Symptoms, and Treatment.


Application explains what must happen for a creature to suffer from the poison: is it ingested, breathed, or do you simply touch it?


Symptoms breaks down the poison’s symptoms in increments of time. Slow-acting poisons might have day-by-day symptomatic progressions, while fast acting ones might be minute-by-minute or even round-by-round.


Treatment explains what one must do to become cured of the poison. This typically involves some kind of antidote, magical healing, and/or successful INT/Medicine checks.

DISEASE

“Disease” is a term used loosely in this context to refer to any sort of pathogenic alien entity that enters one’s body. This could technically be a disease, infection, or virus, but all three are simply referred to as diseases for the purposes of game mechanics.


Diseases are categorized in the same way as poisons, with Rarity, Lethality, and Transmissibility. As with poisons, Lethality and Transmissibility are not referenced in the Core Rulebook, but will come into play in later releases.


Disease stat blocks have five parts: Infection, Outbreak, Contagion, Symptoms, and Treatment.


Infection explains what must happen for a creature to become infected.


Outbreak is an optional tool that an MC can use when facilitating the outbreak of a disease.


Contagion explains how quickly the disease might spread, and how to determine whether it spreads to certain heroes or relevant NPCs.


Symptoms convey on an hour-by-hour or day-by-day basis what happens to an infected creature.


Treatment informs you on the steps you must take to cure a disease. This will typically involve making multiple INT/Medicine checks, and certain expensive ingredients or magical healing abilities might make this process easier or might be required to even attempt this process.

BEASIAN BOILS

Very Rare Disease

Lethality: Medium

Transmissibility: High


Infection: A creature gains Beasian Boils when they are hit with a Blood Beast’s Rip and Tear ability.


Outbreak and Contagion: Beasian Boils spread when Blood Beasts or their spawn hit with specific attacks, so creatures only contract this disease through combat.


Symptoms: An infected creature gains the following symptoms at the start of each of the following days (after Taking a Rest, if they were able to Rest). The effects are cumulative.


  • Day 1: Become Sickened as brown-green boils blister up just beneath your skin. Some might burst and ooze fluorescent lime green gunk.
  • Day 2: You gain Iron Will and Steel Mind as a fog takes hold in your brain. You also cannot benefit from the Focused or Rallied conditions.
  • Day 3: While in combat, when you are Bloodied, you have Bleeding (1d4) as more of your pustules split open. Coarse hair begins sprouting along your body.
  • Day 4: Make an END check. If you roll above a 17, you gain Fatigue and make this roll again on the following day.

If you roll below a 17 on or after Day 4, you transform into a Blood Beast Spawn. As a Blood Beast Spawn, you have the stats of a Dire Hyena and can no longer distinguish ally from enemy. When you hit someone with your Bite attack, make a secondary attack: +3 vs FORT. Hit: The creature contracts Beasian Boils.


Treatment: Curing the Beasian Boils requires one or more INT/Medicine checks. Each check must include herbs and bandages soaked in medicinal salves mixed with silver shavings. Each application of this medicine costs at least 80 sc, which the process consumes. You cannot receive more than 1 check per 24 hours.


The DC to treat this disease is 17. If you receive treatment within 24 hours of being hit, you are cured. Otherwise, you need to receive two successful treatments to be cured.


If you are a Blood Beast Spawn, Greater Restoration and similar magic can remove the disease and restore you to your previous state.

BOIL PLAGUE

Common Disease

Lethality: Low

Transmissibility: Extreme


Infection: Boil Plague spreads through physical contact. Creatures that suffer Boil Plague break out in running sores whose oils carry infection.


Outbreak: 5d20 creatures in an urban area contract Boil Plague.


Contagion: At the end of each day, Xd20 additional creatures are infected, where X equals the number of days since the outbreak. Subtract 1 die from this roll (to a minimum of 0d20) for every 20 infected creatures that have remained at least partially quarantined for the past 24 hours and have received adequate hygienic care (washing the sores, keeping hands clean, etc.). If all infected creatures are partially quarantined and receive adequate hygienic care, the disease does not spread.


If you need to determine whether a specific PC or NPC contracts the disease, make an attack against that creature’s FORT. The attack bonus is +1 per 1 minute the creature has spent in close proximity to one or more infected creatures, to a maximum of +20.


Symptoms: A creature gains progressively worse symptoms the longer that they are infected. Once a creature is infected, they become infectious and can spread the disease 1d3 hours later. Once the creature is infectious, then it gains the following symptoms at the start of each of the following days. These effects are cumulative.


  • Day 1: Shaken
  • Day 2: Sickened
  • Day 3: Staggered
  • Day 4: Remove the Staggered condition
  • Day 5: Remove the Sickened condition
  • Day 6: Remove the Shaken condition
  • Day 7: Creature fully recovers

Creatures that suffer Boil Plague and recover have nasty pockmarks that rarely fade completely, but fade to being barely noticeable after 2d12 months. Creatures that suffer Boil Plague and recover become immune to Boil Plague for 1 year.


Treatment: Treating a creature with the Boil Plague requires a DC 15 INT/Medicine check. If you succeed, then the creature’s symptoms rapidly progress. Over the next hour, that creature’s symptoms advance by 1d3 days. A creature can benefit from this treatment only once while infected with Boil Plague.

DEATHBOIL PLAGUE

Rare Disease

Lethality: High

Transmissibility: Medium


Infection: Deathboil Plague spreads through physical contact. An infected creature’s boils and pustules will frequently burst, making treatment a messy process. These burst oils spread rapidly and infect other creatures that come into contact with them.


Outbreak: 5d10 creatures in an urban area contract Deathboil Plague.


Contagion: At the end of each day, Xd10 additional creatures are infected, where X equals the number of days since the outbreak. Subtract 1 die from this roll (to a minimum of 0d10) for every 10 infected creatures that have remained completely quarantined for the past 24 hours. If all infected creatures are quarantined, the disease does not spread.


If you need to determine whether a specific PC or NPC contracts the disease, make an attack against that creature’s FORT. The attack bonus is +1 per 5 minutes the creature has spent in close proximity to one or more infected creatures, to a maximum of +20.


Symptoms: A creature gains progressively worse symptoms the longer that they are infected. Roll 1d3. A creature that is exposed to the disease takes that many days to incubate. Once they have incubated, the disease is potent and can spread to other creatures. Additionally, once the creature has incubated, then it gains the following symptoms at the start of each of the following days. These effects are cumulative.


  • Day 1: Shaken.
  • Day 2: Sickened.
  • Day 3: Slowed.
  • Days 4-14: Gain Fatigue. During this period, infected creatures have blotches of blackened or otherwise discolored skin.
  • Days 11-14: If a creature is not cured by day 10 or later, it must lose a randomly determined limb in order for the treatment to be successful.

Creatures that suffer Deathboil Plague and recover often have terrible scars for the rest of their lives, including divots in their skin where their necrosis was carved off of their body.


Treatment: Treating a creature with the Deathboil Plague requires a DC 20 INT/Medicine check. If you succeed, then the creature’s symptoms do not progress for the day. If you treat the same creature two days in a row and are successful both times, then the creature is cured and is immune to Deathboil Plague for 1 year.

DEEPSHROOM POISONING

Rare Disease

Lethality: High

Transmissibility: Low


Infection: When a creature inhales a Deepshroom Hive’s cloud of spores, it becomes infected with Deepshroom Poisoning.


Symptoms: An infected creature gains the following symptoms at the start of each of the following days (after Taking a Rest, if they were able to Rest). The effects are cumulative.


  • Day 1: Demoralized.
  • Day 2: Shaken.
  • Day 3: Sickened.
  • Days 4-6: Gain Fatigue.
  • Day 7: The creature dies.
  • Day 7+1d4: The creature rises as a Deepshroom Shambler.

Treatment: Curing Deepshroom Poisoning requires one or more INT/Medicine checks. Each check must include herbs and medicinal salves worth at least 30 sc each, which the process consumes. A creature cannot receive more than 1 check per 12 hours. When a creature makes this check, note the numerical result and add it to any previous checks from this instance of Deepshroom Poisoning. When the numerical results reach a cumulative total of 30, the infection is cured.

FLESHLING FEVER

Rare Disease

Lethality: High

Transmissibility: Low


Infection: M’l’ykkri Flesh Swarms and M’l’ykkri Flesh Worms can both cause a creature to gain Fleshling Fever.


Symptoms: An infected creature gains the following symptoms at the start of each of the following hours. The effects are cumulative.


  • Hour 1: Demoralized.
  • Hour 2: Slowed.
  • Hours 3-12: Gain Fatigue.
  • Upon Death: Become a M’l’ykkri Fleshling and 2d4 M’l’ykkri Flesh Worms emerge from the corpse.

Treatment: Curing Fleshling Fever requires one or more INT/Medicine checks. Each check must include herbs and medicinal salves worth at least 20 sc each, which the process consumes. A creature cannot receive more than 1 check per 30 minutes. When a creature makes this check, note the numerical result and add it to any previous checks from this instance of Fleshling Fever. When the numerical results reach a cumulative total of 75, the infection is cured.


When the infection is cured, 2d4 M’l’ykkri Flesh Worms emerge from the healed body.

GIFT OF THE CHOSEN

Rare Disease

Lethality: High

Transmissibility: Medium


Infection: A creature might contract Gift of the Chosen if exposed to another infected individual.


Outbreak: If an outbreak occurs, a ghost ship appears. When 20 or more creatures have a form of Chosen Madness in a single settlement of 200 or more individuals, roll percentile dice at dusk each night. On a roll of 76-100, a ghost appears in port between dusk and dawn each morning. It is crewed by 5d4 Chosen Crewmates, 1d4 Chosen Boatswains, and 1 Chosen Captain. While a ghost ship is in the port or the settlement, a dense fog covers the whole settlement. This fog persists so long as the ghost ship is in port and cannot be removed by magical means. So long as this fog persists, creatures looking for the settlement must succeed on a DC 16 INT/Navigating check in order to find it.


Contagion: Any humanoid within 10 ft of an infected individual while they have the Insane Babbling symptom or while proselytizing must make a DC 15 CHA check or gain one Madness symptom from the following table. Anyone who reads a scroll or tome infected with Gift of the Chosen must make a DC 18 CHA check or immediately become infected and make 3 Chosen Madness table rolls.


These tomes and scrolls are common within some cults.


Symptoms: 24 hours after infection, a creature must roll 1d6 and gain a permanent effect from the following Chosen Madness table. This roll must be repeated for every additional 24 hours of infection. If a creature rolls an effect that it already has, it gains no additional madness for that day.


If a creature gains all six symptoms from the Chosen Madness table, it is immediately transformed into a Chosen Crewmate.


  • 1 - Uncontrollable Weeping/Laughing.
  • 2 - Terrifying Hallucinations.
  • 3 - Extreme Paranoia.
  • 4 - Masochistic.
  • 5 - Insane Babbling.
  • 6 - Blissful Delirium.

Treatment: Curing Gift of the Chosen requires one or more DC 18 INT/Religion or INT/Medicine checks. An infected individual needs X successful checks, where X equals the number of Chosen Madness symptoms that they suffer from. This treatment can be attempted once every 12 hours. Greater Restoration and similar magic immediately cures the Gift of the Chosen without additional checks, and Lesser Restoration and similar magic counts as two successful checks.

SCARLET SICKNESS

Rare Disease

Lethality: High

Transmissibility: High


Infection: A creature might contract Scarlet Sickness if an open wound is exposed to blood from another creature. Once a creature is infected, their blood becomes infectious regardless of if other creatures have open wounds or not.


Outbreak: 1d4 creatures gain Scarlet Sickness.


Contagion: At the end of each day, all creatures exposed to Scarlet Sickness must make a DC 15 END check. On a fail, a creature gains Scarlet Sickness.


Symptoms: A creature exposed to Scarlet Sickness becomes symptomatic and infectious 1d4 hours after contracting the sickness. Once a creature is infectious, incubated, then it gains the following symptoms at the start of each of the following hours. These effects are cumulative unless noted otherwise.


  • Hour 1: Sickened.
  • Hour 2: Fatigued.
  • Hour 3: Bleeding (1d4)
  • Hour 4: Bleeding (1d6) - this replaces the hour 3 symptom.
  • Hour 5: Bleeding (1d8) - this replaces the hour 4 symptom.
  • Hour 6: Bleeding (1d10) - this replaces the hour 5 symptom.
  • Hour 7: Bleeding (1d12) - this replaces the hour 6 symptom.

Creatures that suffer Scarlet Sickness and recover often have terrible laceration scars for the rest of their lives.


Treatment: Treating a creature with the Scarlet Sickness requires a DC 25 INT/Medicine check. If you succeed, then the creature’s symptoms do not progress for the hour. If you successfully treat the same creature two hours in a row, then the creature is cured and is immune to Scarlet Sickness for 6 months.

STONEROT

Uncommon Disease

Lethality: Low

Transmissibility: High


Infection: A creature gains Stonerot when they are hit with a Sickening Statue’s Infectious ability.


Outbreak and Contagion: Stonerot only spreads through direct contact, so creatures only contract this disease through combat or through failing an attribute check.


Symptoms: An infected creature first undergoes an asymptomatic incubation period. Roll 1d6+1; after that many days, the symptoms begin appearing. An infected creature gains the following symptoms at the start of each of the following days (after Taking a Rest, if they were able to Rest). The effects are cumulative.


  • Days 1-3. You are Slowed. Your skin around the area of exposure looks hard, cracked, and gritty, like rough rock.
  • Day 4-6. You are Frozen. The skin infection spreads and your joints stiffen.
  • Days 7-10. You are Staggered as the infection overtakes most of your body.
  • Days 11-13. You are Petrified.
  • Day 14. You lose any conscious retention of who you are and become a Sickening Statue. You can no longer distinguish friend or foe, you no longer need to eat, drink, or sleep, and you lurk and wait for easy prey to whom you can spread the disease.

Treatment: Curing Stonerot requires one or more INT/Medicine checks. Each check must include medicinal salves or holy water worth at least 10 sc each, which the process consumes. You cannot receive more than 1 check per 24 hours.


When a creature makes this check, note the numerical result and add it to any previous checks from this instance of Stonerot. When the numerical results reach a cumulative total of 100, the infection is cured. If cured, you regain memories of your life prior to becoming a Sickening Statue after 1d4 days.

Vampirism

Uncommon Disease

Lethality: Extreme

Transmissibility: High


Infection: A creature gains Vampirism when they are bitten three times in the span of 24 hours by one or more vampires.


Symptoms: An infected creature gains the following symptoms at the start of each of the following days. The effects are cumulative.


  • Day 1: Become Demoralized.
  • Day 2: You become irritable and lash out at minor provocations (no mechanical effect).
  • Days 3 on until Cured: Gain Fatigue.
  • Upon Death: Become a vampire (see the following “Becoming a Vampire” section).

Treatment: Curing Vampirism requires a combination of divine healing and herbal concoctions. A creature may only be cured of Vampirism before dying; once they die and become a vampire, this is an irreversible condition.


For a creature to be cured of Vampirism, they must be the recipient of the Lesser Restoration or Greater Restoration divine ritual. If they received Lesser Restoration, then they must also receive two doses of a special potion; if they received Greater Restoration, then they only need to receive one dose. A creature may not benefit from both Lesser Restoration and Greater Restoration for the purposes of overcoming this infection.


The potion consists of a blend of garlic and holy water. After grinding down and blending the ingredients, a creature must boil them together and then feed them to the infected creature while the liquid is still simmering. This deals 1d4 fire damage to the creature.


To successfully make the potion, the creature blending together the ingredients may make a DC 14 INT check using the Alchemy or Medicine skills. A player that makes a compelling case for another skill (such as Arcana, Cooking, or Religion) may use it at the MC’s discretion, though using a different skill raises the DC to 16.


A creature may only receive one portion of the treatment (Lesser Restoration or Greater Restoration, and the potion doses) at a time and must Take a Rest between each treatment.


Becoming a Vampire: Once a hero becomes a vampire, the hero becomes an NPC. An NPC that becomes a vampire gains the stats of a Vampire Spawn or Vampire at the MC’s discretion.

ZOMBIE PLAGUE

Uncommon Disease

Lethality: Extreme

Transmissibility: High


Infection: A creature gains Zombie Plague when they are hit with a Zombie’s bite attack and the secondary infection attack that comes with it.


Outbreak and Contagion: Zombie Plague does not spread through living creatures, so has no Outbreak or Contagion rules.


Symptoms: An infected creature gains the following symptoms at the start of each of the following days (after Taking a Rest, if they were able to Rest).


The effects are cumulative.


  • Until Treated: Gain Fatigue twice.
  • Upon Death: Become a Zombie in 1d4 hours.

Treatment: Curing the Zombie Plague requires one or more INT/Medicine checks. Each check must include herbs and medicinal salves worth at least 20 sc each, which the process consumes. You cannot receive more than 1 check per 24 hours.


When a creature makes this check, note the numerical result and add it to any previous checks from this instance of zombie plague. When the numerical results reach a cumulative total of 40, the infection is cured.

INSANITY

Beyond the shelter of civilization, untold horror awaits in the dark recesses of the wilderness. Sometimes, the blackened alleys of an oppressed city and hidden basements of mad scientists hold horrors just as foul. Adventuring often brings heroes face to face with terrifying monsters and maddening experiences.


When your heroes come face to face with abject terror, you might make them roll for insanity. Insanity should primarily change a character’s outlook on the world and be a roleplay tool, although the worst effects of insanity will also have mechanical detriments attached. Be mindful of your players’ yellow and red zones when implementing any of these effects.


Depending on the type of horror that confronts the heroes, you might ask each hero to make an AWR, INT, or CHA check. The higher their roll, the more minor the insanity effect, and a very high roll might allow a hero to shrug off any insanity altogether. The lower the roll, the worse or longer-lasting a hero’s insanity will be.


When assigning DCs to the various effects of insanity, you can use this table as a quick reference, adjusting the DCs up and down as you feel is appropriate for the insanity.
