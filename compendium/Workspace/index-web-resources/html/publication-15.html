You can track your adventurers’ Goodwill in a handful of different ways. If you want to be very granular, use the following table for each town that the heroes spend time in. If you do not want to emphasize Goodwill as much in your game, then just use the “Overall Goodwill” column and only track your heroes’ Goodwill in each region or even in each nation, rather than in individual towns.

Because MythCraft campaigns typically span several years, tracking Goodwill on a granular level can be more rewarding for the adventurers as they see their reputations change and (ideally) improve over the course of the story.

This is especially relevant if you run a campaign rife with political intrigue, skulduggery, and villains that wield the soft power of influence as deftly as a cutpurse wields a knife. Tracking Goodwill more closely can lead to rewarding narratives as your players might become discredited in one area and spend an entire adventure arc reestablishing their credibility.

Tracking a hero’s Goodwill across individual occupations will also naturally lead to different heroes becoming the face of the party in different circumstances.

AWARDING GOODWILL

Along with a monetary reward, cool magic items, and titles and their accompanying prestige, Goodwill can be a great reward when a quest is completed successfully. While a player might not know their exact Goodwill score, they should know that their standing in the eyes of society was improved thanks to their contribution to the town’s safety and/or prosperity.

LANGUAGES

MythCraft does not officially track languages, but assumes that any humanoid can find some way of accurately communicating very basic ideas and simple conversations with other humanoids, either via translator or nonverbal cues. If you want to add a little more complexity to your games, you can use one or more of the following models for adding languages to your game.

LINEAGE LANGUAGES

This model approaches languages through the lens of lineages. Each sapient lineage has its own tongue, and there is a common language shared by all. This model is usually human-centric, assuming that the human tongue is the common language, so you can maintain this trope or invert it and, for example, choose that Halfling is the common tongue.

If you use this model, then in character creation, each hero gets to know two languages: the common tongue and their own lineage language. If a lineage’s own tongue is considered the common tongue, then a hero of that lineage knows any one additional language of the character’s choice.

LEARNING LANGUAGES

As an optional rule if you use languages in your game, you might allow heroes to learn 1 additional language for every 2 points of INT that they have.

Alternatively, you might introduce a special Languages skill with the INT and knowledge tags. Heroes could make Languages checks in order to decipher the intent of a creature speaking a language that they don’t know. Additionally, for every 4 skill points a hero has in this skill, you could allow them to learn another language.

If your world has a large number of languages, you might consider allowing your heroes to learn another language for every 1 point of INT that they have, or for every 2-3 points of the Languages skill, or both.

In addition to the languages spoken by individual lineages, you might introduce languages associated with the Five Sources of Magic or with the various pantheons. You might also introduce languages spoken by humanoid creatures in the Creature Compendium, such as centaurs and clikkiri.
