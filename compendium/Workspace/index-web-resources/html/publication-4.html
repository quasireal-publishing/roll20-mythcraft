USING THIS BOOK

Each subsequent chapter of this book focuses on a different component of designing adventures.

Chapter 2 offers tools for designing NPCs of all kinds, from simple merchants to world-conquering villains.

Chapter 3 suggests different styles of long-term campaigns and provides a few tools for considering your campaign as a whole and breaking it down into manageable adventure arcs.

Chapter 4 provides information for running social encounters and exploration encounters. It also has guidelines for designing skill challenges that you can use in either situation.

Chapter 5 includes detailed guidelines for designing balanced (or unfair) combat encounters and what to consider when doing so.

Chapter 6 provides a handful of threats and dangers beyond simple combat, such as poisons, siege equipment, diseases, and debilitating injuries.

Chapter 7 offers a hoard of magical items and some pointers for designing your own.

Chapter 8 offers tables and mechanisms for customizing your monsters or designing your own creatures to terrify your players.

Chapter 9 is a lore-focused chapter. It leads you through the many nations of Gathandia, the largest continent on Ancerra. It also guides you through the planes of existence, offering many adventure settings for you to pull from.

Chapter 10 contains a short, fully developed adventure designed for low-level adventurers. You might use this as the beginning of your campaign, or you could play through this adventure to get a feel for the game system before you make your own campaign from scratch.

Appendix A contains numerous tables that you can use to quickly generate random NPCs by giving them a name and a very basic personality.

Appendix B has a complete list of skills that heroes might learn.

Appendix C has a few quick reference guides and important rules to help speed up the flow of combat.

HEROES

The adventurers that your players present to you are the main characters of your story. They should be the focus of your campaign, and you should seek to elevate them as the protagonists.

A space smuggler with a laser gun does not belong in a world that has not yet discovered electricity. VILLAINS

It is often said that a story is only as great as its villain. It is usually best to have one or more clear villains in mind when you conceptualize your campaign. This can change once you actually start playing the campaign, as your players might take the story in a direction that steers you away from using the villain that you had originally planned. Even so, having a few clear antagonists in mind will help you plan the milestones that your adventurers might achieve over the game. These principles also work for considering a new antagonist if your party takes the campaign in an unexpected direction.

What are the villainâ€™s driving motives?

Is the villain supposed to be sympathetic or redemptive in any way?

When creating your villains, take some time to consider how they might act and react in different situations. What might they say or do when your campaign reaches key milestones? Using the Values, Drives, and Quirks at the end of this chapter can help you create much more nuanced and realistic villains. Think about what motivates your villain and what event in their past led them down the path that they are on now. Even if the villain never tells their story to the heroes, it will be very helpful for you to know why they make the decisions that they do. Consider a list of things that will trigger them to act in certain ways, or events and circumstances that might give them pause or even make them reconsider their actions.
