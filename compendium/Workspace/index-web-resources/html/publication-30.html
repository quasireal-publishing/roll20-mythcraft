STONEROT

Uncommon Disease

Lethality: Low

Transmissibility: High

Infection: A creature gains Stonerot when they are hit with a Sickening Statues Infectious ability.

Outbreak and Contagion: Stonerot only spreads through direct contact, so creatures only contract this disease through combat or through failing an attribute check.

Symptoms: An infected creature first undergoes an asymptomatic incubation period. Roll 1d6+1; after that many days, the symptoms begin appearing. An infected creature gains the following symptoms at the start of each of the following days (after Taking a Rest, if they were able to Rest). The effects are cumulative.

Days 1-3. You are Slowed. Your skin around the area of exposure looks hard, cracked, and gritty, like rough rock.

Day 4-6. You are Frozen. The skin infection spreads and your joints stiffen.

Days 7-10. You are Staggered as the infection overtakes most of your body.

Days 11-13. You are Petrified.

Day 14. You lose any conscious retention of who you are and become a Sickening Statue. You can no longer distinguish friend or foe, you no longer need to eat, drink, or sleep, and you lurk and wait for easy prey to whom you can spread the disease.

Treatment: Curing Stonerot requires one or more INT/Medicine checks. Each check must include medicinal salves or holy water worth at least 10 sc each, which the process consumes. You cannot receive more than 1 check per 24 hours.

When a creature makes this check, note the numerical result and add it to any previous checks from this instance of Stonerot. When the numerical results reach a cumulative total of 100, the infection is cured. If cured, you regain memories of your life prior to becoming a Sickening Statue after 1d4 days.

Vampirism

Uncommon Disease

Lethality: Extreme

Transmissibility: High

Infection: A creature gains Vampirism when they are bitten three times in the span of 24 hours by one or more vampires.

Symptoms: An infected creature gains the following symptoms at the start of each of the following days. The effects are cumulative.

Day 1: Become Demoralized.

Day 2: You become irritable and lash out at minor provocations (no mechanical effect).

Days 3 on until Cured: Gain Fatigue.

Upon Death: Become a vampire (see the following Becoming a Vampire section).

Treatment: Curing Vampirism requires a combination of divine healing and herbal concoctions. A creature may only be cured of Vampirism before dying; once they die and become a vampire, this is an irreversible condition.

For a creature to be cured of Vampirism, they must be the recipient of the Lesser Restoration or Greater Restoration divine ritual. If they received Lesser Restoration, then they must also receive two doses of a special potion; if they received Greater Restoration, then they only need to receive one dose. A creature may not benefit from both Lesser Restoration and Greater Restoration for the purposes of overcoming this infection.

The potion consists of a blend of garlic and holy water. After grinding down and blending the ingredients, a creature must boil them together and then feed them to the infected creature while the liquid is still simmering. This deals 1d4 fire damage to the creature.

To successfully make the potion, the creature blending together the ingredients may make a DC 14 INT check using the Alchemy or Medicine skills. A player that makes a compelling case for another skill (such as Arcana, Cooking, or Religion) may use it at the MCs discretion, though using a different skill raises the DC to 16.

A creature may only receive one portion of the treatment (Lesser Restoration or Greater Restoration, and the potion doses) at a time and must Take a Rest between each treatment.

Becoming a Vampire: Once a hero becomes a vampire, the hero becomes an NPC. An NPC that becomes a vampire gains the stats of a Vampire Spawn or Vampire at the MCs discretion.

ZOMBIE PLAGUE

Uncommon Disease

Lethality: Extreme

Transmissibility: High

Infection: A creature gains Zombie Plague when they are hit with a Zombies bite attack and the secondary infection attack that comes with it.

Outbreak and Contagion: Zombie Plague does not spread through living creatures, so has no Outbreak or Contagion rules.

Symptoms: An infected creature gains the following symptoms at the start of each of the following days (after Taking a Rest, if they were able to Rest).

The effects are cumulative.

Until Treated: Gain Fatigue twice.

Upon Death: Become a Zombie in 1d4 hours.

Treatment: Curing the Zombie Plague requires one or more INT/Medicine checks. Each check must include herbs and medicinal salves worth at least 20 sc each, which the process consumes. You cannot receive more than 1 check per 24 hours.

When a creature makes this check, note the numerical result and add it to any previous checks from this instance of zombie plague. When the numerical results reach a cumulative total of 40, the infection is cured.

INSANITY

Beyond the shelter of civilization, untold horror awaits in the dark recesses of the wilderness. Sometimes, the blackened alleys of an oppressed city and hidden basements of mad scientists hold horrors just as foul. Adventuring often brings heroes face to face with terrifying monsters and maddening experiences.

When your heroes come face to face with abject terror, you might make them roll for insanity. Insanity should primarily change a characters outlook on the world and be a roleplay tool, although the worst effects of insanity will also have mechanical detriments attached. Be mindful of your players yellow and red zones when implementing any of these effects.

Depending on the type of horror that confronts the heroes, you might ask each hero to make an AWR, INT, or CHA check. The higher their roll, the more minor the insanity effect, and a very high roll might allow a hero to shrug off any insanity altogether. The lower the roll, the worse or longer-lasting a heros insanity will be.

When assigning DCs to the various effects of insanity, you can use this table as a quick reference, adjusting the DCs up and down as you feel is appropriate for the insanity.
