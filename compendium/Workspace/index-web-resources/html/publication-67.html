UNJAARN

Once a nation of nearly twice its current size, Unjaarn has many layers of history in its large cities crumbling under their own weight. The young nation of Vadan used to be part of Unjaarn, and while Unjaarn maintains its traditional customs, Vadan is much more driven by change and new philosophies. This leads most Unjaarni to disregard the Vadanians as foolish.

Swampy and humid year-round, the people of Unjaarn are almost all at least competent in boating through bogs and marshes. The wilderness of Unjaarn is a tangle of mangroves, hemlock, and black spruce among other tropical and subtropical trees, all of which is overgrown with lichens, kudzu, and parasitic plants like strangler figs. All manner of dangerous animals and strange monsters lurk in the murky waters outside of civilization, but skilled rangers can navigate the swamps skillfully to avoid the worst of these creatures.

Academic. Unjaarn’s largest cities have well-equipped schools of philosophy, science, and fine arts. Attendance at such schools is reserved for the top three castes. Trades workers commonly apprentice under masters to learn their crafts.

Unjaarni Castes. Unjaarn maintains a rigid caste system. An Unjaarni will remain in the same caste that their parents were born into, as will their children, in perpetuity. Some Unjaarni believe that this system helps foster internal peace as nobody has to compete for higher status: their status is preordained. Other Unjaarni think that this limits the potential creativity and competence from many of the lower castes and the system should change. Needless to say, a majority of the Unjaarni that are happy with the system as

it currently stands are those in the upper castes, although no group holds completely monolithic views. Many Unjaarni that are completely unsatisfied with the caste system will emigrate to Vadan, which has the same cultural history but has much more social mobility.

Brahma. The first and highest caste of Unjaarn are its religious leaders. While they do not hold any governmental authority, the other castes look to them for moral guidance and their counsel often sways the Nelida.

Nelida. The second caste are the elite among the Lectuadi. There are only a few dozen Nelida families in Unjaarn; they are regional and national legislators, judges, and generals.

Lectuadi. The third caste is the lowest caste allowed (and in fact required) to attend formal schooling. Local legislators and judges come from this caste. Lectuadi are also appointed to tactical roles overseeing the Duskiran.

Duskiran. The fourth caste is the armed forces. Both local town guards and the national infantry are considered Duskiran. Captains and generals are from the fourth caste, however.

While many Likishuan and Kirish attempt to emigrate to Vadan for upward social mobility, Unjaarn is also in a heated conflict with Vadan. The border between the two nations is a violent and contentious place.

Kirish. The trades workers and merchants share the fifth caste. Blacksmiths, tanners, weavers, and traders live slightly more comfortable lives than the Likishuan, but are still forbidden from pursuing formal schooling.

Likishuan. The sixth and lowest caste of Unjaarn is the manual laborers, who maintain the nation’s agriculture and infrastructure. Even the lowest caste is treated with basic humanoid dignity, as the higher castes recognize that it is the Likishuan who keep the nation functional.

The Likishuan and Kirish castes have a dialect of speaking that is almost incomprehensible to the upper castes. While the words are generally the same as the common language, the accent and speech patterns are so thick that the Likishuan and Kirish can easily communicate private messages to one another.

Religious. At religious ceremonies, the castes are made to sit in order of their caste, with the Likishuan furthest from the altar, the Nelida closest to it, and the Brahma performing rituals that involve a sacrificial offering of food and drink to the gods.

Unjaarni believe that every pantheon exists, and likely many more that nobody is aware of. Each town and household may have specific deities that they feel especially drawn to worship as their patrons. The Shibmar Conclave of the Archfae - specifically Lob, Tamat, and Khailain - are nationally venerated and believed to be some of the most powerful gods out of any pantheon. Most Unjaarni believe in reincarnation, though they do not scientifically understand Essence.

Their caste system seems strange to me, but it has worked for thousands of years. Perhaps there are lessons to learn from their culture.

-Finneas

Mercantile and Trade. Unjaarn exports many potent spices, artistic clay goods, and flavorful foods. It also exports colorful pigments that make bright dyes. Unjaarn is locked in economic conflict with Vadan, each trying to outmaneuver the other in international trade.

Underworld. There are individuals in every caste who are willing to break convention. A thinly spread but well organized network of smugglers help people cross the border to Vadan when desired.

NELIDI

The capital of Unjaarn is a massive city spread across several small islands in a shallow area of the swamplands. With a population of over three million, Nelidi is the largest city on the east of the Cloudpiercer Range.

The largest Brahma temple is in Nelidi, along with multiple elite schools and the palace from which the Nelida run their nation.

POINTS OF INTEREST

Swamplands of the Great Chasm. See Buyleist for more information.

A member of the Nelida privately confers with the heroes and conspires to abolish the caste system.

A beloved Brahma has gone missing. When the heroes venture into the swamp, they find that they have teleported to the Everwilds.

VADAN

A network of swampy bayous and steamy marshlands, Vadan recently seceded from its southern neighbor, Unjaarn. Although they share a culture and belief system, the Vadanians differ strongly from the Unjaarni in their approach to governance. While Unjaarn is a rigid caste system, Vadan runs on an aggressively capitalist government. Many folk from Vadan would prefer to live in Unjaarn, and vice versa, but the border between the two nations is a dangerous and often violent region.

Academic. Vadan’s capital has a large university, but very few scholars reside in it year-round, which makes it difficult for the university to grow.

Mercantile and Trade. Vadan exports food, spices of many varieties, and clay to neighboring nations. They also mine the Great Chasm to their north and export gems and rare metals. Vadan is locked in a trade war with Unjaarn, trying to lock down trade partnerships with other nations and block Unjaarn from doing so.

Militant. The Vadanian military is tremendously patriotic but poorly trained. Having recently secured independence from Unjaarn, the Vadanian military now struggles to bring discipline and structure to its ranks. In skirmishes with Unjaarni, the Vadanians usually win in battles that transpire in thick underbrush but lose if the battle takes place on an open lake or plain.

Political and Social. Vadan has a very free-market approach to governance. Those who seize on risky opportunities might flourish or might end up being left out to dry. There is no social safety net other than the generosity of neighbors, and many would rather ensure that they get ahead instead of spending time offering charity and risking getting dragged down themselves. Because of Vadan’s highly stressful and volatile system, some Vadanians emigrate to Unjaarn, where the rigid caste system provides stability.

Their economy has totally run amok, and there is poverty everywhere you look. I’m not sure that Unjaarn has the answers either, but in Vadan, thousands of people go to bed hungry every night. Something has to change.

-Chauncey

Religious. Vadanians largely share the same religious beliefs as the Unjaarni. There are many hundreds of gods, and individual communities may pay special homage to specific deities. As in Unjaarn, three specific gods from the Shibmar Conclave of the Archfae hold special prominence: Tamat, Khailain, and Immort.

Underworld. Many independent smugglers help transport people south into Unjaarn when they wish. Some of the most successful smugglers know which Unjaarni officials can be bribed to help secure a higher caste for the immigrant.
